# Study Smart: Cornell Notes + Google NotebookLM

A guide for students on how to take better notes, organize your thinking, and use AI tools to study more effectively.

---

## Why This Matters

Here's the truth: **re-reading your notes is one of the least effective ways to study.** Research shows that students who actively organize and question their notes retain significantly more than those who just highlight and re-read.

Cornell Notes give you a system for organizing your thinking *while* you learn. Google NotebookLM lets you turn those organized notes into a personal AI tutor that only knows what *you've* studied — so it can quiz you, explain things differently, and help you find gaps in your understanding.

The workflow is simple: **Take smart notes → Feed them to NotebookLM → Study with AI that knows your material.**

## Part 1: Cornell Notes

### What Are Cornell Notes?

Cornell Notes split your page into three sections that serve different purposes:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                              │
│  CUE COLUMN          │   NOTE-TAKING AREA    │
│  (narrow, ~2.5 in)   │   (wide, ~6 in)       │
│                       │                       │
│  Questions you want   │   Your actual notes   │
│  answered             │   from class, reading │
│                       │   or the activity     │
│  Key vocabulary       │                       │
│                       │   Use short phrases,  │
│  Main ideas           │   diagrams, examples  │
│                       │                       │
│  "Triggers" that      │   Skip lines between  │
│  help you recall      │   different ideas     │
│  the content          │                       │
│                       │                       │
├───────────────────────┴───────────────────────┤
│                                              │
│  SUMMARY (2-3 sentences)                     │
│  In your own words, what's the main takeaway │
│  from this page of notes?                    │
│                                              │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Three Sections Explained

**Cue Column (Left Side — Fill in AFTER class)**

This is the most important part, and most students skip it. After you take notes, go back and write questions or keywords that match each section of your notes. These become your self-test prompts.

Good cues:
- "What are the 3 subatomic particles?"
- "How is atomic number different from mass number?"
- "Why do noble gases not react?"
- Key vocabulary: "isotope," "ion," "valence"

Bad cues:
- "Page 47" (not a question, doesn't trigger recall)
- "Important" (too vague)
- Copying the heading from the textbook

**Note-Taking Area (Right Side — Fill in DURING class)**

This is where you write during the lesson, video, or reading. Some tips:
- Use your own words, not word-for-word copying
- Leave space between ideas so you can add to them later
- Use abbreviations you understand (→ for "leads to", ∴ for "therefore", w/ for "with")
- Draw diagrams, arrows, and simple sketches — visuals stick better than text
- Star or mark things that confused you so you can follow up

**Summary (Bottom — Fill in AFTER class)**

Write 2-3 sentences that capture the main point. The act of summarizing forces you to process what you learned. If you can't summarize it, that's a signal you need to review.

### Cornell Notes for Science Classes

Science has some specific patterns that work well with Cornell Notes:

| Cue Column Prompt | Note-Taking Area |
|---|---|
| What did we observe? | Describe the demonstration, lab result, or data |
| Why does this happen? | Explanation, mechanism, or theory |
| How do I calculate/apply this? | Formula, steps, worked example |
| How does this connect to ___? | Links to previous topics or real-world applications |
| What vocabulary is new? | Definitions in your own words with an example |

### Using SBAR Tags as Cue Prompts

If your Learning Map uses SBAR tags, you can use them to structure your cues:

- **KU (Know & Understand):** "What is ___?" "Define ___." "List the steps of ___."
- **TT (Think & Transfer):** "Why does ___?" "What would happen if ___?" "Compare ___ and ___."
- **C (Communicate):** "How would I explain ___ to someone?" "What evidence supports ___?"

This aligns your notes directly with how you'll be assessed.

## Part 2: Taking Cornell Notes Digitally

You can use Cornell Notes on paper or digitally. Here are your options:

### Option A: Google Docs (Recommended for NotebookLM)

1. Create a new Google Doc
2. Insert a 2-column table for the cue and notes columns
3. Add a full-width row at the bottom for the summary
4. Title the doc with the topic and date (e.g., "Atomic Structure — Jan 15")

**Pro tip:** Create one Google Doc template and make copies for each lesson. Keep them all in one Drive folder called "Cornell Notes — [Subject]."

### Option B: Paper Notes, Then Photograph

1. Take notes on paper using the Cornell format
2. Use Google Drive's scan feature (the camera icon in the Drive app) to scan your notes
3. The scan creates a PDF in your Drive that NotebookLM can read

### Option C: Google Docs with Headings (Simplest)

If the table feels clunky, use this structure instead:

```
# Topic: Atomic Structure — Jan 15

## Questions & Cues
- What are the three subatomic particles?
- How do you read the periodic table to find proton/neutron count?
- What is an isotope?

## Notes
Atoms are made of protons (positive, in nucleus), neutrons (neutral, in nucleus),
and electrons (negative, orbiting). The atomic number = number of protons...

## Summary
Atoms have three parts. The atomic number tells you the protons, and from there
you can figure out electrons and neutrons. Isotopes are atoms of the same element
with different neutron counts.
```

This format is clean, easy to type, and NotebookLM handles it well.

## Part 3: Google NotebookLM

### What Is NotebookLM?

Google NotebookLM is an AI research tool that reads documents *you* upload and lets you ask questions, generate study guides, and create quizzes based only on *your* materials. It doesn't search the internet — it only uses what you give it.

This is what makes it powerful for studying: the AI can only help you with what you've actually studied. No random internet answers. No hallucinated facts. Just your notes, explained back to you in different ways.

### Getting Started

1. Go to [notebooklm.google.com](https://notebooklm.google.com)
2. Sign in with your school Google account
3. Click **"New Notebook"**
4. Give it a clear name (e.g., "Chemistry Unit 1 — Atomic Structure")

### Adding Your Notes as Sources

Click **"Add Source"** and choose from:
- **Google Docs** — Select your Cornell Notes docs directly from Drive
- **Google Slides** — If you have class presentation notes
- **PDFs** — Scanned handwritten notes or teacher handouts
- **Web URLs** — Links to specific resources from your Learning Map hexes
- **Copied text** — Paste in notes from other apps

**Best practice:** Add all your Cornell Notes for one unit into a single notebook. This gives the AI the full picture of what you've learned.

### What You Can Do With NotebookLM

Once your sources are uploaded, try these:

**Ask Questions About Your Notes**
- "What are the key differences between ionic and covalent bonds based on my notes?"
- "Explain the concept of electron configuration using simpler terms."
- "What connections exist between atomic structure and the periodic table in my notes?"

**Generate Study Materials**
- Click **"Study Guide"** to get a structured overview of your notes
- Click **"Briefing Doc"** for a condensed summary
- Click **"FAQ"** to see common questions and answers drawn from your material
- Click **"Timeline"** if your notes cover a sequence of events or processes

**Create Practice Quizzes**
- Ask: "Create a 10-question quiz based on my notes about chemical bonding"
- Ask: "Give me 5 application questions that test whether I can transfer my knowledge"
- Ask: "What are the hardest concepts in my notes? Quiz me on those."

**Generate Audio Overviews**
- Click **"Audio Overview"** to generate a podcast-style conversation about your notes
- Listen while commuting, exercising, or doing chores — this is passive review that actually works because it's based on *your* material

**Find Gaps in Your Understanding**
- Ask: "Based on my notes, what topics seem incomplete or need more detail?"
- Ask: "Are there any contradictions in my notes?"
- Ask: "What questions should I be able to answer based on these notes that I haven't addressed?"

### The Study Workflow

Here's a complete cycle you can use for each unit:

```
During Class / Activity
    │
    ▼
Take Cornell Notes (Note-Taking Area)
    │
    ▼
After Class (within 24 hours)
    │
    ├── Fill in Cue Column (questions & keywords)
    ├── Write Summary (2-3 sentences)
    │
    ▼
Before Studying
    │
    ├── Upload notes to NotebookLM
    ├── Generate a Study Guide
    ├── Review gaps the AI identifies
    │
    ▼
Active Study Session
    │
    ├── Cover the Note-Taking Area, use Cues to self-test
    ├── Ask NotebookLM to quiz you
    ├── Ask NotebookLM to explain confusing parts differently
    │
    ▼
Before the Assessment
    │
    ├── Generate a practice quiz in NotebookLM
    ├── Listen to the Audio Overview for review
    └── Focus on the gaps NotebookLM identified
```

## Part 4: Tips for Success

### The 24-Hour Rule

Review and complete your cue column and summary within 24 hours of taking notes. After that, you forget a large portion of what you heard in class. The cue column is what turns passive notes into an active study tool.

### Quality In = Quality Out

NotebookLM is only as helpful as the notes you feed it. Sloppy, incomplete notes produce vague, unhelpful AI responses. Detailed, well-organized Cornell Notes produce specific, useful study materials. This is actually a great motivator for taking better notes — you immediately see the difference in what the AI can do for you.

### Don't Just Read — Interact

The biggest mistake students make with NotebookLM is passively reading the study guide it generates. Instead:
- Quiz yourself first, then check against the study guide
- Ask the AI to explain things you got wrong in a different way
- Challenge the AI: "I think ___ because ___. Am I right?"

### One Notebook Per Unit

Keep your notebooks organized by unit, not by class period. This way each notebook contains a complete set of notes on one topic, and the AI can make connections across all your lessons for that unit.

### Use Your Learning Map Hexes

Each hex on your Learning Map represents a concept or activity. After completing a hex:
1. Take Cornell Notes on what you learned
2. Include the hex name and any standards/skills listed
3. Note connections to other hexes you've already completed

This builds a web of understanding that mirrors the Learning Map itself.
